# Paleobiogeographic and Biostratigraphic Implications of the Identification of Ganoid Scales from the Upper Cretaceous and Lower Tertiary of South America

**Authors:** Mireille Gayet & François J. Meunier
**Published:** Documents des Laboratoires de Géologie, Lyon, n°125, 1993, pp. 169-185
**Original Language:** French

---

## Abstract

Recent discoveries in South America of ganoid fish remains belonging to Holosteans (Semionotiformes and Lepisosteiformes) and to Brachiopterygians (Polypteriformes) allow us to better define the paleobiogeography of these groups, more precisely delimit their supposed ages of appearance and disappearance, and provide supplementary data on South American paleogeography.

**Keywords:** South America, ganoid fish, Upper Cretaceous, Lower Tertiary, Paleobiogeography, Biostratigraphy

---

## Introduction

During the period of interest (Upper Cretaceous/Lower Tertiary), ganoid-scaled fishes are relatively rare compared to preceding periods and are limited to:
- **Polypteriformes**
- Two orders of Holosteans:
  - **Semionotiformes**
  - **Lepisosteiformes**

## Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) Analysis

### Tubercle Characteristics

The external ganoine surface presents numerous microscopic tubercles (small rounded-tip cones, not visible to naked eye) that provide **taxonomic signatures**.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    TUBERCLE CHARACTERISTICS                          │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                      │
│  Key observations:                                                   │
│                                                                      │
│  1. For a given species, tubercle DIAMETER is very CONSTANT          │
│     with low variability                                             │
│                                                                      │
│  2. For a given individual, DIAMETER and DENSITY are constant        │
│     regardless of scale position on body (flank, back, tail)         │
│                                                                      │
│  3. For a given species, DIAMETER and DENSITY do not vary            │
│     according to animal age (tested on Polypterus bichir             │
│     at 120mm and 160mm)                                              │
│                                                                      │
│  4. Regenerated scales show IDENTICAL tubercle patterns              │
│     to original scales (tested on Calamoichthys calabaricus)         │
│                                                                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Function of Tubercles

According to Meunier et al., these tubercles likely serve a **mechanical function**: adhesion of an organic layer from the sub-epidermal basal membrane, creating an "anti-slip structure" that minimizes epidermis sliding on the ganoine during water flow over the integument during swimming.

## Paleobiogeographic Implications

### Semionotidae

The discovery of *Lepidotyle enigmatica* in the Maastrichtian of Bolivia and Argentina **extends the extinction date** of this family in South America. This form was probably:
- Marine, or
- Capable of migrating between marine and freshwater environments

The ganoine remodeling observed in most scales suggests environmental modification:
1. Migration from calcium-rich (marine) to calcium-poor (freshwater) environment, OR
2. Massive freshwater influx from the continent into a restricted marine environment

**Either interpretation requires the presence of a sea or arm of the sea nearby.**

### Polypteridae: Gondwanan Origin

The presence of Polypteridae in South America (*Dagetella sudamericana*) indicates an **origin prior to separation of Africa and South America**, i.e., at least **-110 Ma**.

The absence of Polypteridae in India (if confirmed as historical rather than artifact) suggests the origin of this order at **-148 to -165 Ma** (date of India-Africa separation).

---

## Conclusions

1. **Semionotiformes:** Study still incomplete, but presence in Bolivia extends their disappearance date to at least Maastrichtian. *Lepidotyle* provides evidence for marine connection.

2. **Lepisosteiformes:** Well known due to extant representatives. Scale-based reference scale allows placement of fragmentary Cretaceous/Tertiary species. Combining vicariance and dispersal makes paleobiogeography possible and plausible.

3. **Polypteriformes:** Presence in South America surprising but logical given Middle Cretaceous African record. The Polypteridae family appears to be **Gondwanan** in origin.
